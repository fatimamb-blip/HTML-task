Biography

Franz Kafka (1883â€“1924) was a German-speaking writer, widely regarded as one of the major figures of 20th-century literature. His works, including "The Metamorphosis," "The Trial," and "The Castle," explore themes of alienation, existential anxiety, and the absurdity of modern life. Kafka's unique style and vision have influenced countless writers and thinkers, and his legacy endures as a symbol of literary modernism.

Born into a middle-class Jewish family in Prague, Kafka had a difficult relationship with his domineering father. He studied law at Charles University and worked for an insurance company while pursuing his writing in his spare time. Despite his relatively short life, he produced a body of work that would profoundly influence 20th-century literature and philosophy.

Most of his work was published posthumously by his friend Max Brod, who defied Kafka's dying wish to burn his manuscripts. This act of literary preservation gave the world some of the most important works of modern literature.

Impact on Literature

Kafka's influence extends far beyond literature. The term "Kafkaesque" has entered common usage to describe surreal, nightmarish situations characterized by complex and bureaucratic systems. His exploration of themes such as guilt, anxiety, and the individual's struggle against impersonal forces continues to resonate with readers worldwide.

  • Pioneered modernist literature
  • Influenced existentialism and absurdism
  • Created the concept of "Kafkaesque" storytelling
  • Inspired countless authors, filmmakers, and artists
  • Works translated into over 40 languages
